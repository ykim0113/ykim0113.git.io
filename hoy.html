Hoy

Focus on today to accomplish long-term goals.

Overview

During my sophomore year summer break, I came across my friend’s problem which I emphasized a lot with as a fellow college student: he was worried about becoming a Junior. He has been using productivity apps to stay organized but he said they didn’t help him too much to accomplish his long term goals, especially during the semester.

I wanted to help him contextualize the problem to come up with a viable solution. The initial outcome was to build a productivity app for long-term goals and for me was to design user-friendly features and design system of the app’s UI.

User Research

I spent a week researching about potential users of the app. Although the primary user of the app was my friend (Computer Science major at Georgia Tech), I wanted to broaden our users to students at Georgia Tech as 67.5% of students uses productivity apps. My research encompassed of survey and market research.

Survey

I asked my teammate and couple more friends about their experiences of using a productivity app or planner through a Google Form to collect qualitative data. Some questions I asked were:

  • Past experience using productivity apps

  • How user would define long-term and short-term goal (duration vs frequency)

  • How users manage their tasks (prioritizing, categorizing, number of lists)

Market Research

I conducted a market research on existing productivity and time management apps. From this research I was able to determine that our app would be a combination of project management and productivity apps.

Ideation

From the insights, two big design choices I made were focusing on the usability of the app and designing a UI element system that would reflect these design choices.

Design Challenge 1: Usability of the app

  • Put milestones and tasks for the day on a single list for the day so the user can have a holistic view of their day and not switch back and forth screens with only milestones and with only tasks.

  • Use detailed logs for a long-term goal to decrease the user’s burden of having to remember where they left off in the previous milestone.

Design Challenge 2: UI Elements Design

As I was the only UI/UX designer of the app, I was responsible for designing the whole UI components of the app. Following Material Design guideline, I customized the component so they will be consistent across the entire app and align with the mood I wanted to capture. Over two months, I came up with three design themes as I was developing the lo-fi wireframe.

To capture a clean, calm, organized feeling for the user experience of the app, I used:

  • White and light blue color scheme

  • Roboto typeface to ensure legibility and readability

  • Logo for the starting screen to complete the branding of the app

Takeaways

1. Use consistent terms.

When communicating with each other as a developer and a designer, my friend and I were often confused in the beginning because we kept switching terms to describe the concept of long- and short-term in the app. We had very different understanding of what these terms meant and even had to schedule multiple meetings just to clarify these terms. If we decided to use one set of consistent terms to describe the feature of the app in the beginning, we wouldn't have went through this confusion.

2. Working remotely.

My friend and I worked from the States to Korea. I was grateful for my friend of staying flexible with his schedule and actively communicating over Slack when our times matched. Thus to make most of our time, I took notes of our meetings and created a summary (notes, visualizations) for future meetings.

As my first UI/UX project, it was challenging to learn and apply UI/UX design concepts on my own along the way. However, I enjoyed learning the end-to-end design process and pushing myself to improve the design and features of the app over multiple iterations. I was also happy to be able to work on my first design challenge project as a team.
